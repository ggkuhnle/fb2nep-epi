1 FB2NEP Assessment 1 — Marking Rubric

Total marks: 100 + 5 bonus Section A (25 marks) + Section B (75 marks)

1.1 Section A — Conceptual epidemiology (25 marks)

1.1.1 A1 — Dietary assessment method for SSB intake (6 marks)

What a good answer includes:

  • Names a specific, plausible method (e.g. food frequency questionnaire, 24-hour dietary recall, food diary, beverage-specific questionnaire)
  • Explains why it is appropriate for habitual SSB intake (repeated exposure, population-level surveillance, practicality)
  • Acknowledges at least one limitation or trade-off
Marks Descriptor
5–6 Appropriate method selected with clear, epidemiologically sound justification. Limitation acknowledged. Concise and precise.
3–4 Correct method identified; justification present but partial or generic. Limited engagement with trade-offs.
1–2 Method vague or partially correct; justification weak or incorrect.
0 No relevant answer, or method is clearly inappropriate with no justification.

Notes:

  • FFQ is the most defensible choice for habitual intake in a large cohort; 24-h recall, DD are also acceptable if justified (e.g. multiple recalls or NDNS-style use)
  • bonus for commenting on problems with self-reporting

1.1.2 A2 — Type of censoring in epidemiological studies (6 marks)

What a good answer includes:

  • Names and correctly defines one type of censoring (right censoring is most common and expected; interval or left censoring also acceptable)
  • Gives a concrete epidemiological example relevant to the study context (e.g. loss to follow-up, end of study period, competing event)
  • Explains what censoring means for the analysis (the event had not occurred by the time of last observation)
Marks Descriptor
5–6 Correct type named and defined. Clear, contextually appropriate example. Consequence for analysis noted.
3–4 Correct type with reasonable definition. Example present but generic or slightly off.
1–2 Type named but definition confused or example absent/wrong.
0 No relevant answer, or fundamental misunderstanding of censoring.

Marker notes:

  • Right censoring is the standard expected answer
  • “Administrative censoring” (end of study) is acceptable as a specific instance of right censoring
  • Do not accept “missing data” as an equivalent to censoring without further clarification

1.1.3 A3 — DAG for SSB intake → CVD risk (7 marks)

What a good answer includes:

  • A diagram with directed arrows (hand-drawn is acceptable if scanned/photographed)
  • SSB as the exposure, CVD risk (or equivalent) as the outcome, with a direct arrow SSB → CVD
  • At least two plausible variables on the diagram (e.g. SES, obesity/BMI, smoking, age, sex, physical activity)
  • Correct directionality of arrows (e.g. SES → SSB; SES → CVD; not the reverse)
  • No directed cycles
Marks Descriptor
6–7 Plausible DAG with exposure, outcome, and at least two additional correctly placed variables. Arrows directionally correct. No cycles. Brief explanation if needed.
4–5 Exposure and outcome present with direct path. Additional variables included but one directional error or missing node.
2–3 Basic exposure → outcome arrow present. Few or no additional variables, or errors in directionality.
0–1 Absent, unreadable, or fundamentally incorrect (e.g. reversed arrows throughout, undirected graph).

Marker notes:

  • Award marks for a plausible structure even if it differs from the “model” answer, provided arrows are causally defensible
  • A DAG showing obesity as a mediator (SSB → BMI → CVD) alongside direct effect (SSB → CVD) is good epidemiological reasoning
  • Do not penalise for omitting variables that are not in the dataset, as long as the graph is internally consistent

1.1.4 A4 — One confounder with causal reasoning (6 marks)

What a good answer includes:

  • Identifies one specific variable (e.g. SES, smoking, physical activity, age)
  • Explicitly states the three confounding criteria: (1) associated with SSB intake, (2) associated with CVD risk, (3) not on the causal pathway from SSB to CVD
  • Uses causal language (not just statistical association)
Marks Descriptor
5–6 Correct variable identified. All three criteria addressed with causal reasoning. Clear and concise.
3–4 Correct variable. Two of three criteria addressed. Reasoning present but partially statistical rather than causal.
1–2 Plausible variable named but reasoning absent, circular, or incorrect.
0 No relevant answer, or variable chosen is a mediator or collider presented as a confounder.

Marker notes:

  • SES and smoking are the strongest answers given the dataset structure
  • Physical activity is acceptable — it affects both SSB intake and CVD risk and is not on the causal path
  • Age and sex are also defensible

1.2 Section B — Data analysis and interpretation (75 marks)

1.2.1 B1 — Table 1 and epidemiological commentary (18 marks)

What the question asks:

Compare obese and non-obese participants across all variables in Table 1. Report the population name. Write a commentary of approximately 150 words.

Table construction (6 marks)

Marks Descriptor
5–6 Table 1 present and correctly structured. Mean ± SD for continuous variables; n (%) for categorical. Both groups shown. Population name recorded.
3–4 Table present but minor errors (e.g. wrong summary statistic for one variable, missing a variable). Population name present.
1–2 Table incomplete, poorly formatted, or population name absent.
0 Table absent or entirely incorrect.

Epidemiological commentary (12 marks)

Marks Descriptor
10–12 Commentary identifies key differences between groups (higher SSB, lower PA, higher CVD risk in obese group; SES and smoking patterns). Uses epidemiological framing (not just “obese people have higher X”). Notes at least one potential data quality consideration or caveat.
7–9 Most major differences identified. Some epidemiological reasoning. Minor omissions or imprecision.
4–6 Some differences noted but commentary is mainly descriptive with little interpretation.
1–3 Very brief or only partially correct. No epidemiological reasoning.
0 Commentary absent or irrelevant.

Marker notes:

  • The population name must appear somewhere in the submitted document
  • Do not penalise for dataset-specific variation in effect sizes (the dataset is individually generated)
  • Award credit for noting that Table 1 differences do not prove causation

1.2.2 B2 — Distributions and transformation justification (15 marks)

What the question asks:

Describe the distributions of ssb, bmi, and cvd_risk. Justify any transformations or categorisations.

Distribution description (6 marks — 2 per variable)

Marks Descriptor
2 Distribution accurately described (e.g. right-skewed, approximately normal, bimodal). Key feature noted (outliers, floor effect, etc.).
1 Partially correct or vague (e.g. “not normal” without description).
0 Absent or wrong.

Apply per variable: ssb, bmi, cvd_risk.

Transformation/categorisation decisions (9 marks — 3 per variable)

Marks Descriptor
3 Decision stated (transform / do not transform / categorise) with clear epidemiological or statistical justification. Reference to model assumptions or interpretability.
2 Reasonable decision with partial justification.
1 Decision stated but justification weak, missing, or contradicted by described distribution.
0 Absent or incoherent.

Marker notes:

  • ssb is right-skewed; log-transformation is well-justified. Categorisation (low/medium/high) is also acceptable with justification.
  • bmi is approximately normal; transformation is generally not required, though obese (binary) has already been created.
  • cvd_risk is approximately symmetric (logistic-sigmoid bounded 0–100); transformation is generally not needed.

1.2.3 B3 — Regression model and interpretation (30 marks)

What the question asks: Fit and interpret the pre-specified linear regression model (cvd_risk ~ ssb + obese + age + sex + ses + smoking). Interpret effect of SSB, obesity, age, sex, and discuss SES and smoking as potential confounders.

Regression output present and correctly fitted (4 marks)

Marks Descriptor
4 Model output (coefficient table with estimates, CIs, p-values) present and matches specified formula.
2–3 Output present but incomplete (e.g. CIs missing, formula slightly different).
0–1 Output absent or wrong model fitted.

Interpretation of SSB effect (8 marks)

Marks Descriptor
7–8 Effect estimate reported (β, direction, magnitude). Confidence interval discussed. P-value contextualised. Clear statement of “per unit increase in SSB servings/day, CVD risk increases by X points, holding other variables constant.” Uncertainty acknowledged.
5–6 Effect correctly reported and interpreted but uncertainty (CI) underemphasised or p-value over-relied upon.
3–4 Direction correct, magnitude mentioned, but interpretation imprecise or lacking conditional framing (“holding other variables constant”).
1–2 Effect reported numerically but interpretation absent, reversed, or confused with OR/HR.
0 Absent or entirely wrong.

Interpretation of obesity effect (5 marks)

Marks Descriptor
4–5 Coefficient for obese correctly interpreted: mean difference in CVD risk for obese vs non-obese, adjusted for other covariates. Magnitude and direction stated.
2–3 Correct direction, partial interpretation. Adjustment not mentioned.
0–1 Absent or incorrect (e.g. interprets as OR).

Interpretation of age and sex effects (5 marks)

Marks Descriptor
4–5 Both age and sex effects correctly interpreted with direction and magnitude. Notes that these are adjusted estimates.
2–3 One of the two correctly interpreted, or both partially correct.
0–1 Absent or incorrect.

Discussion of SES and smoking as confounders (8 marks)

Marks Descriptor
7–8 Discusses whether SES and/or smoking coefficients change after inclusion in the model (compared to a simpler model or expected direction). Uses causal reasoning — not just statistical significance. Comments on the plausibility of confounding given the DAG.
5–6 Both variables mentioned. Confounding discussed but reasoning is mostly statistical (“the p-value changed”) rather than causal.
3–4 One variable discussed, or both mentioned superficially.
0–2 Absent, or confounding defined/applied incorrectly.

Marker notes:

  • Individual β values will vary by dataset — mark reasoning and interpretation, not specific numbers
  • Do not penalise for using summary() output rather than the concise table, as long as the correct values are reported
  • Penalise if the student reports ORs or HRs when the model is linear regression

1.2.4 B4 — DAG-informed adjustment strategy (12 marks)

What the question asks: Use the Part A DAG to justify the adjustment set in B3. Identify one variable that should not be adjusted for and explain why using the DAG.

Justification of adjustment set (6 marks)

Marks Descriptor
5–6 Correctly argues which variables in the model are confounders (open backdoor paths) and should be included. References the DAG structure explicitly. Distinguishes between confounders and other variable roles.
3–4 Correct variables identified as requiring adjustment. Limited DAG reference or reasoning partially correct.
1–2 Some correct variables mentioned but adjustment strategy not clearly justified or DAG not used.
0 Absent or incorrect (e.g. recommends adjusting for all variables without reasoning).

Identification of a variable not to adjust for (6 marks)

Marks Descriptor
5–6 Variable correctly identified (e.g. obese/BMI as a mediator on the SSB → CVD path, or a collider if the student has constructed the DAG appropriately). Role (mediator or collider) named and explained using the DAG. Correct consequence stated (adjusting would block the causal pathway / introduce bias).
3–4 Correct variable identified, role stated, but explanation of consequence incomplete or slightly confused.
1–2 A plausible variable named without adequate causal reasoning, or role incorrectly assigned.
0 Absent, or student argues for adjusting for a mediator/collider without recognising the problem.

Marker notes:

  • The strongest answer for “should not adjust” is obese/BMI as a mediator (SSB → BMI → CVD), as this is supported by the dataset’s generating model
  • If the student’s DAG from Part A placed a variable as a collider, accept that variable here if their reasoning is internally consistent
  • Award credit for internal consistency with the student’s own DAG, even if it differs from the model answer

1.3 Optional Bonus — Robustness of findings (up to 5 marks)

Two options available: Bonus A (stratified analysis) or Bonus B (variable transformation).

Marks Descriptor
5 Additional analysis correctly performed. Results compared to main analysis with epidemiologically meaningful interpretation. Explains why the analytical choice is informative. Clear and concise (≤ 150 words).
3–4 Analysis present and mostly correct. Interpretation reasonable but lacks depth or clear comparison with main findings.
1–2 Analysis attempted but results misinterpreted or reasoning absent.
0 Not attempted, or analysis present with no interpretation.

Marker notes:

  • Bonus marks are awarded for reasoning, not for technical sophistication
  • A simple and well-interpreted stratified analysis scores higher than a complex analysis with no interpretation
  • Do not award bonus marks if the main analysis (B3) is absent
